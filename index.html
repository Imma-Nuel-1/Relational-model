Relational Model

Relationships in the Diagram

There are four relationships in the diagram:

  • One-to-Many (Hotel to Room, Hotel to Employee)
  • Many-to-One (Room to Hotel, Employee to Hotel)
  • One-to-One (Room to Type, Room to Category)
  • One-to-Many (Employee to Employee_Speciality)

1. One-to-Many (1..N) Relationships

This relationship occurs when one record in a table is associated with multiple records in another table. Examples from the model:

  • Hotel to Room: One hotel can have many rooms. This is represented by the foreign key Hotel_Id in the Room table.
  • Hotel to Employee: One hotel can have many employees. This is represented by the foreign key Hotel_Id in the Employee table.

2. Many-to-One (N..1) Relationships

This is the inverse of the one-to-many relationship. It indicates that multiple records from one table point to a single record in another table. Examples:

  • Room to Hotel: Many rooms belong to one hotel. This is represented by the foreign key Hotel_Id in the Room table.
  • Employee to Hotel: Many employees belong to one hotel. This is represented by the foreign key Hotel_Id in the Employee table.

3. One-to-One (1..1) Relationships

This relationship occurs when one record in a table is associated with exactly one record in another table. Examples:

  • Room to Type: Each room belongs to exactly one type. This is represented by the foreign key Type_Id in the Room table.
  • Room to Category: Each room belongs to exactly one category. This is represented by the foreign key Category_Id in the Room table.

4. One-to-Many (1..N) or Many-to-One (N..1) Relationships (Employee to Employee_Speciality)

This relationship captures the specialties of employees:

  • Employee to Employee_Speciality: One employee can have multiple specialties, or you could view it as many specialties related to one employee. This is represented by the foreign key Employee_Id in the Employee_Speciality table.
